Nazare: The 100-Foot Wave Quest

Nazare, Portugal continues to be ground zero for big wave surfing's most ambitious pursuit: riding a 100-foot wave. The Nazare Canyon, an underwater geological formation stretching 170 miles into the Atlantic, focuses open ocean swells into towering walls of water that have already produced waves exceeding 80 feet. Scientists at the University of Lisbon estimate that the canyon's bathymetry is capable of producing faces beyond 100 feet under optimal conditions.

The 2025-2026 winter season has already delivered several massive swells to Nazare's Praia do Norte. A December event saw estimated wave faces of 70-75 feet, with multiple surfers completing successful rides. The key factors for a record attempt include a deep low-pressure system tracking across the North Atlantic with sustained winds above 50 knots over a fetch of at least 1,000 miles, combined with a long-period swell interval of 18 seconds or greater.

Infrastructure improvements at Nazare have made the venue safer for extreme sessions. The new jet ski staging area, upgraded medical facilities, and permanent camera installations allow for real-time wave measurement during sessions. The Portuguese Maritime Authority has implemented a tiered access system that restricts water access during the largest swells to only pre-approved safety teams.

Jaws and Hawaiian Big Wave Scene

Pe'ahi, known as Jaws, on Maui's north shore remains the proving ground for big wave surfing performance. While Nazare produces the largest waves by pure measurement, Jaws delivers the most powerful and hollow big wave barrels on earth. The WSL Big Wave Awards consistently recognize Jaws rides for their combination of size, commitment, and technical difficulty.

The 2026 season has seen renewed interest in paddle-in surfing at Jaws, where surfers catch waves under their own power rather than being towed by jet skis. Billy Kemper, Kai Lenny, and Nathan Florence have pushed the limits of what's possible without motorized assistance, catching waves in the 40-50 foot range through pure paddling fitness and positioning.

Hawaii's outer reefs, including Peahi, Himalayas off Kauai, and Phantoms near Oahu, are producing rideable waves with increasing frequency as Pacific storm tracking technology improves. Better swell models allow surfers to position themselves at the right break on the right day with unprecedented accuracy.

Mavericks and Cold Water Giants

Mavericks in Half Moon Bay, California, continues to challenge the world's best in frigid 50-degree water with sharks patrolling below. The break's unique rock shelf creates a wedging effect that can double wave height in seconds, making it one of the most dangerous waves on earth. The Mavericks Surf Awards, relaunched in 2024, have reinvigorated competition at this iconic break.

Northern California's 2026 winter has been exceptional, with multiple XXL swells generated by successive atmospheric river events. The combination of heavy rainfall, strong winds, and northwest swell has created some of the largest conditions seen at Mavericks in a decade. Local chargers like Grant Washburn and Peter Mel have been joined by international visitors seeking to add Mavericks to their big wave resumes.

Emerging cold water big wave spots are gaining attention. Mullaghmore Head in Ireland and Shipstern Bluff in Tasmania have both produced waves rivaling traditional big wave venues. As more surfers explore remote coastlines with advanced swell forecasting tools, predictions suggest that new world-class big wave locations will be discovered by 2028.

Athletes to Watch in 2026

Kai Lenny continues to redefine what's possible in big wave surfing through his multi-discipline approach. His foil surfing and tow-in innovations have opened new possibilities for wave selection and ride length. Lenny's 2025 XXL Biggest Wave nomination for a ride at Jaws confirmed his status as the sport's most versatile waterman.

Lucas Chianca, known as Chumbo, from Brazil has established himself as the most fearless surfer at Nazare. His commitment to riding the heaviest waves on the biggest days sets him apart from the field. Chianca's deep relationship with local fishermen and safety crews gives him an information advantage about ocean conditions that visiting surfers simply cannot replicate.

On the women's side, Justine Dupont of France remains the undisputed queen of big wave surfing. Her 73-foot ride at Nazare in 2023 still stands as one of the greatest achievements in surfing history. Dupont's methodical preparation, which includes breath-hold training, heavy water swimming, and detailed study of Nazare's swell patterns, represents the professional standard for big wave athletes.

Technology Changing Big Wave Surfing

Artificial intelligence is transforming big wave surfing safety and performance. Machine learning models trained on decades of buoy data, satellite imagery, and local observation can now predict wave heights at specific breaks with accuracy within 10% up to 72 hours in advance. This allows surfers and safety teams to prepare with unprecedented precision.

Wearable technology including impact vests with integrated GPS, heart rate monitors, and inflation systems has dramatically reduced the risk of drowning. Modern inflation vests can be triggered manually or automatically when sensors detect a surfer has been held underwater for a dangerous duration. These devices have been credited with saving multiple lives at Nazare and Jaws since their widespread adoption in 2023.

Drone technology has revolutionized both safety and documentation in big wave surfing. Real-time aerial views allow safety teams to track surfers' positions in the impact zone with precision that was impossible from water-level jet skis alone. High-resolution drone footage has also become the primary tool for official wave measurement, replacing the inconsistent photographic methods previously used by the WSL and Guinness World Records.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the biggest wave ever surfed?

Sebastian Steudtner holds the Guinness World Record for the largest wave ever surfed, riding an estimated 86-foot wave at Nazare, Portugal in 2024. The measurement was verified using drone footage and scientific analysis by a team of oceanographers.

Where are the biggest waves in the world?

Nazare in Portugal consistently produces the largest surfable waves, with faces exceeding 80 feet during major winter swells. Jaws on Maui, Mavericks in Northern California, and Cortes Bank off San Diego also produce massive waves during peak swell events.

When is big wave season?

Big wave season in the Northern Hemisphere runs from October through March, peaking in December through February. Southern Hemisphere big wave spots like Shipstern Bluff in Tasmania fire during the austral winter from June through August.

How do surfers survive big wave wipeouts?

Modern big wave surfers use inflation vests that provide buoyancy when triggered, breath-hold training to survive extended hold-downs, and extensive safety teams with jet ski rescue crews. Training programs include underwater rock running, pool blackout drills, and simulation of high-stress decision-making scenarios.

Will someone surf a 100-foot wave?

Scientists and professional surfers believe a 100-foot wave will be surfed at Nazare within the next few years. The canyon's geometry can produce waves of that size during exceptional swell events. The challenge is not just wave size but the extreme speed, turbulence, and danger at that scale.
