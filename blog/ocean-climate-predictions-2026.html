ENSO Forecast 2026: El Nino, La Nina, or Neutral?

The El Nino-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) is the single most important climate driver for prediction markets across multiple domains. ENSO affects global weather patterns, ocean temperatures, agricultural yields, energy demand, storm tracks, and -- critically for predict.surf -- wave conditions at surf breaks around the world.

As of early 2026, NOAA's Climate Prediction Center and international forecasting agencies are monitoring the tropical Pacific closely. The ENSO cycle has been in a transitional phase following the strong El Nino of 2023-2024 and the subsequent La Nina that developed through 2024-2025. The key question for 2026 is whether the tropical Pacific will return to El Nino conditions, settle into ENSO-neutral, or continue with La Nina.

Current ENSO State and Model Forecasts

The Nino 3.4 index -- the primary measure of ENSO state, based on sea surface temperatures in the central equatorial Pacific -- has been trending toward neutral as of early 2026. The major ENSO forecast models show a split:

  • Statistical models (CPC ENSO regression, others): Favor ENSO-neutral conditions through mid-2026, with a slight lean toward El Nino developing by the fall.
  • Dynamical models (ECMWF, NCEP CFSv2): Show a wider range of outcomes. Several ensemble members develop moderate El Nino by Q3 2026, while others maintain neutral conditions.
  • Japanese MRI model: Has been more bullish on El Nino development, showing warming in the central Pacific beginning in the spring.

What El Nino Would Mean for 2026

If El Nino develops in 2026, the effects would be wide-ranging:

  • North Pacific surf: El Nino typically strengthens the North Pacific jet stream, generating more frequent and powerful storms that send large northwest swell to Hawaii, California, and Mexico. Expect bigger surf at Pipeline, Mavericks, and all West Coast breaks.
  • Southern California: El Nino years historically bring increased rainfall, larger surf, and more beach erosion to Southern California. The WSL Finals at Trestles could see above-average swell -- changing the competitive dynamics.
  • South Pacific: El Nino shifts convective activity eastward, potentially reducing tropical cyclone activity in the western Pacific while increasing it in the eastern Pacific. This affects swell generation for Tahiti, Fiji, and other South Pacific surf destinations.
  • Agriculture: El Nino brings drought to Australia and Southeast Asia while increasing rainfall in South America. These shifts have enormous implications for crop yields and food prices -- relevant to markets on predict.garden.
  • Atlantic hurricanes: El Nino typically suppresses Atlantic hurricane activity through increased wind shear. A strong El Nino in 2026 would likely produce a below-average Atlantic hurricane season -- a tradeable signal for storm prediction markets.

How ENSO Affects Everything: Surf, Agriculture, and Energy

ENSO's reach extends far beyond ocean science. For prediction market traders who operate across the Predict Network's 16 domains, understanding ENSO is like having a master key that unlocks edges in markets spanning multiple categories.

Surf Competition Implications

El Nino and La Nina fundamentally alter the wave climate at CT venues. During El Nino years, the WSL Pipeline event tends to see larger surf because the more active North Pacific storm track generates consistent northwest groundswell. Conversely, La Nina years tend to produce a more typical or slightly below-average Hawaiian surf season, with swell arriving from a more northerly angle.

For the WSL Finals at Trestles, El Nino years historically produce more south swell during the late-summer and early-fall window, potentially creating larger and more powerful conditions than the typical 2-4 foot Trestles fare. This would favor power surfers over pure aerial specialists -- an adjustment most prediction market participants fail to make until the forecast becomes obvious weeks before the event.

Energy Market Connections

ENSO affects energy markets through its impact on temperature and precipitation patterns. El Nino winters tend to be warmer across the northern United States and Canada, reducing heating demand. Conversely, El Nino often brings cooler, wetter conditions to the southern U.S., affecting electricity demand patterns. Traders on prediction markets related to energy prices and consumption can use ENSO forecasts as a baseline adjustment to their models.

Agricultural and Food Price Connections

The agricultural implications of ENSO are among the most well-documented and tradeable. El Nino-driven droughts in Australia reduce wheat and barley production. Increased rainfall in Argentina and Brazil can boost soybean yields. Drought in Southeast Asia reduces palm oil production. These supply shocks propagate into global food prices with predictable lags. Markets on predict.garden that track food prices, crop yields, and agricultural trends are directly informed by ENSO state.

Ocean Temperature Records and Predictions

Global ocean temperatures have been setting records at an alarming pace. The year 2023 saw unprecedented ocean heat content, and subsequent years have continued near or above those record levels. For prediction markets, ocean temperature is both a direct market (will temperatures set new records?) and an input to dozens of related markets.

Sea Surface Temperature Trends

The global mean sea surface temperature (SST) has exceeded the 1991-2020 climatological mean by record margins in recent years. As of early 2026, SST anomalies remain elevated, particularly in the North Atlantic, where temperatures have been running well above historical norms since mid-2023.

Prediction markets on ocean temperature records have become highly active:

  • "Will 2026 set a new global SST record?" -- Currently trading at approximately 40% YES. The answer depends heavily on whether El Nino develops, which would add additional warming to an already warm ocean baseline.
  • "Will the North Atlantic SST anomaly exceed +1.5C at any point in 2026?" -- Trading at 55% YES, reflecting the persistent warmth in the Atlantic basin.
  • "Will NOAA declare 2026 the hottest year on record?" -- Trading at 30% YES. This depends on both ocean temperatures and global atmospheric conditions.

What Record Ocean Temperatures Mean

Elevated ocean temperatures have cascading effects that create opportunities across the Predict Network:

  • Coral bleaching: Warm SSTs are the primary driver of mass coral bleaching events. Extended periods above bleaching thresholds (typically 1C above the maximum monthly mean) trigger coral stress and mortality.
  • Storm intensification: Warmer oceans provide more energy for tropical cyclones. While ENSO state affects the number of storms, ocean temperature affects their intensity. Even in a low-activity year, the storms that do form can be unusually powerful if ocean temperatures are elevated.
  • Marine ecosystem shifts: Warmer waters push species poleward, change fish stock distributions, and alter marine food chains. These shifts affect commercial fishing, marine conservation outcomes, and coastal tourism.
  • Surf conditions: Ocean temperature affects wave generation indirectly through its influence on atmospheric circulation patterns. Very warm ocean basins can generate unusual storm tracks that produce unexpected swell at surf breaks.

Sea Level Rise: 2026 Milestones and Long-Term Projections

Global mean sea level has been rising at an accelerating rate, currently approximately 3.6 millimeters per year based on satellite altimetry data. While this may sound small, the cumulative effect and regional variability make sea level rise one of the most consequential climate outcomes for prediction markets.

2026 Sea Level Predictions

Based on current trajectory and accounting for ENSO effects on global sea level (El Nino temporarily increases global mean sea level by several millimeters through thermal expansion), prediction markets are pricing the following:

  • "Will global mean sea level set a new satellite-era record in 2026?" -- 85% YES. Sea level rise is a one-way trend with small interannual variability. New records are almost certain each year.
  • "Will any major U.S. city experience a record-breaking tidal flooding month in 2026?" -- 70% YES. Tidal flooding frequency has been increasing along the U.S. East Coast and Gulf Coast, with records being set regularly.
  • "Will global sea level rise rate accelerate to 4.0 mm/year or higher in 2026?" -- 25% YES. An acceleration above the current trend would require additional ice sheet contributions beyond current rates.

Coastal Impact Markets

Sea level rise creates specific, tradeable outcomes for coastal communities. Prediction markets on predict.surf related to coastal flooding, beach erosion, and infrastructure impacts are informed by sea level data combined with local factors:

  • Tidal flooding events: The combination of sea level rise, astronomical tides, and weather systems produces coastal flooding that can be predicted with reasonable accuracy weeks in advance.
  • Beach nourishment decisions: Coastal communities increasingly invest in beach nourishment projects. Markets on whether specific beach nourishment projects will be approved and funded are influenced by sea level projections and recent erosion rates.
  • Coastal property values: The long-term trajectory of sea level rise is beginning to affect coastal real estate markets in measurable ways. Prediction markets on coastal property trends incorporate sea level data as a key input.

Regional Sea Level Variability Matters

Global mean sea level rise of 3.6 mm/year masks enormous regional variation. The U.S. East Coast experiences higher-than-average rates due to land subsidence and changes in ocean circulation. Some Pacific Island nations face rates exceeding 10 mm/year due to regional oceanographic factors. When trading sea level prediction markets, always check the local rate, not the global average.

2026 Atlantic Hurricane Season Predictions

The Atlantic hurricane season (June through November) is one of the most actively traded climate prediction market categories. Multiple forecasting agencies release pre-season outlooks, and markets reprice throughout the season as conditions evolve.

Pre-Season Outlook

The major forecasting centers have not yet released their official 2026 Atlantic hurricane season forecasts (Colorado State University typically publishes in April), but the key drivers can be assessed now:

  • ENSO state: If El Nino develops by summer 2026, it would suppress Atlantic hurricane activity through increased wind shear. This is the single most important factor for the seasonal forecast.
  • Atlantic SSTs: The persistently warm North Atlantic provides a favorable thermodynamic environment for hurricane development. Warm SSTs alone would support an above-average season.
  • West African monsoon: The strength of the West African monsoon affects the development of African easterly waves, which seed many Atlantic hurricanes. Early indicators suggest a normal to slightly active monsoon for 2026.

Market Pricing for the 2026 Hurricane Season

Based on the competing signals (warm Atlantic favoring activity, potential El Nino suppressing activity), prediction markets are pricing an interesting tension:

  • "Above-average Atlantic hurricane season (>14 named storms)?" -- 45% YES
  • "Major hurricane (Category 3+) makes U.S. landfall in 2026?" -- 30% YES (historical base rate is approximately 25%)
  • "2026 Atlantic ACE (Accumulated Cyclone Energy) exceeds 150?" -- 40% YES

The uncertainty around ENSO development creates a wide range of possible outcomes. If El Nino develops strongly, expect these YES prices to drop significantly. If ENSO stays neutral or La Nina persists, expect them to rise.

Pacific Storm Season and Its Impact on Surf

While Atlantic hurricanes get more media attention in the United States, the Pacific storm season is far more consequential for surf prediction markets. North Pacific winter storms generate the swell that makes Pipeline, Mavericks, and the entire California coast come alive.

North Pacific Storm Track Forecast

The North Pacific storm track in winter 2026-2027 will be heavily influenced by ENSO state. Under El Nino conditions, the jet stream extends farther east across the Pacific, pushing the storm track closer to California and generating more direct west and southwest swell for West Coast breaks. Under La Nina, the jet stream contracts westward, with storms tracking farther north and sending swell at a more northwesterly angle.

For surf prediction markets on predict.surf, the North Pacific storm track determines the entire character of the winter surf season. An El Nino-enhanced storm track means bigger, more frequent swell for California and Hawaii, creating conditions that favor power surfers and tube riders at CT events held during the winter months.

South Pacific Cyclone Season

South Pacific tropical cyclones generate the groundswell that lights up Teahupo'o, Cloudbreak, and other legendary South Pacific reef breaks. The South Pacific cyclone season runs from November to April, with peak activity in January through March. ENSO state shifts cyclone activity eastward during El Nino (more storms near French Polynesia) and westward during La Nina (more storms near Australia and Fiji).

For the WSL Tahiti Pro at Teahupo'o, the ideal scenario is a South Pacific cyclone passing south of French Polynesia, generating large, long-period swell that arrives at the reef as perfectly groomed 8-12 foot barrels. This scenario is more likely during El Nino years, which is one reason why El Nino historically produces more memorable Teahupo'o events.

Coral Bleaching Predictions and Marine Ecosystem Markets

Mass coral bleaching events have become one of the most visible and consequential impacts of ocean warming. For prediction markets, coral bleaching offers clearly defined, measurable outcomes that can be predicted with reasonable accuracy using SST data.

2026 Coral Bleaching Risk Assessment

NOAA's Coral Reef Watch program provides real-time and forecast Coral Bleaching Heat Stress metrics based on satellite SST data. As of early 2026, the elevated ocean temperatures that have persisted since 2023 continue to put reefs at risk.

Key prediction market questions around coral bleaching include:

  • "Will the Great Barrier Reef experience mass bleaching in 2026?" -- Trading at 55% YES. The GBR has experienced four mass bleaching events in seven years, and elevated SSTs in the Coral Sea suggest continued risk.
  • "Will NOAA declare a global coral bleaching event in 2026?" -- Trading at 40% YES. The fourth global bleaching event was declared in 2024, and conditions remain borderline for a continuation or new event.
  • "Will Caribbean coral cover decline by more than 5% in 2026?" -- Trading at 35% YES. Caribbean reefs face thermal stress combined with disease outbreaks that have been devastating coral populations.

Trading Coral Bleaching Markets

Coral bleaching markets are among the most predictable climate markets because the primary driver (ocean temperature) is measured continuously and the bleaching response is well-understood. When SST exceeds the bleaching threshold for a specific reef system for more than 4 weeks, bleaching becomes highly probable. Traders who monitor SST anomalies from Coral Reef Watch can often identify bleaching events 2-4 weeks before they are confirmed by in-water surveys and reported in the media.

Arctic Ice and Polar Predictions for 2026

Arctic sea ice extent is another well-tracked climate metric with active prediction markets. The annual September minimum is the most closely watched data point, and markets on whether 2026 will set a new record low are active year-round.

2026 Arctic Ice Forecast

Arctic sea ice has been declining at approximately 13% per decade since satellite observations began in 1979. The current record low was set in September 2012, and several recent years have come close without breaking it. El Nino conditions can contribute to lower Arctic ice through atmospheric teleconnections that warm the Arctic, potentially pushing 2026 toward record territory if El Nino develops.

Prediction market pricing: "Will 2026 set a new record low for September Arctic sea ice extent?" is trading at approximately 20% YES. This reflects the difficulty of breaking a record set during an unusual combination of factors in 2012, but acknowledges the ongoing long-term decline and potential El Nino influence.

How to Trade Climate Prediction Markets

Climate prediction markets require a different approach than competition or financial markets. The key differences:

Long Time Horizons

Many climate markets resolve over months or years. This means capital is locked up for extended periods. Only commit capital you will not need for the resolution timeframe. The trade-off is that climate markets often have larger mispricings because fewer traders are willing to commit capital for long durations.

Strong Baseline Models

Climate prediction benefits from well-established physical models (ENSO forecasts, SST projections, ice models) that provide rigorous probability estimates. Use these models as your starting point, and only trade when market prices diverge significantly from model-based probabilities. If NOAA's ENSO model says 35% chance of El Nino and the prediction market says 35%, there is no edge -- move on.

Event-Driven Repricing

Climate markets reprice around key data releases: NOAA monthly ENSO diagnostics, weekly SST updates, monthly sea ice extent data, and seasonal hurricane forecast updates. These are scheduled events with known release dates. Position before the data release if you have a view on whether the data will exceed or fall short of consensus expectations.

Cross-Domain Arbitrage

Because ENSO affects markets across multiple domains (surf, agriculture, energy, weather), inconsistent pricing across domains creates arbitrage-like opportunities. If El Nino is priced at 35% on predict.surf but its consequences (bigger North Pacific surf, reduced Atlantic hurricanes) are priced as if El Nino has only a 20% chance, there is a mispricing in the consequence markets.
